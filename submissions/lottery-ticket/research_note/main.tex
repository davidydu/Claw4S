\begin{abstract}
We test the lottery ticket hypothesis on minimal-scale neural networks: can subnetworks identified by pruning at initialization match the performance of their dense counterparts? Using 2-layer ReLU MLPs ($\sim$25K parameters), we sweep 8 sparsity levels (0--95\%) across 3 pruning strategies (magnitude, random, structured) on two synthetic tasks---modular arithmetic (mod 97) and random-features regression. We find that magnitude-pruned subnetworks maintain $>$95\% of dense performance up to 70--80\% sparsity on modular arithmetic and up to $\sim$90\% on regression, confirming that ``winning tickets'' exist even at initialization in tiny networks. Magnitude pruning consistently outperforms random and structured pruning. All 144 training runs complete in under 3 minutes on CPU, and every result is reproduced across 3 random seeds.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The lottery ticket hypothesis (LTH), introduced by Frankle \& Carbin~\cite{frankle2019lottery}, posits that dense neural networks contain sparse subnetworks that, when trained in isolation from their original initialization, can match the full network's accuracy. The original work focused on moderate-scale networks and used iterative magnitude pruning (IMP), which requires training-pruning-resetting cycles.

A natural question is whether ``winning tickets'' can be identified \emph{before any training}---i.e., by pruning at initialization. Prior work on pruning at initialization~\cite{lee2019snip,wang2020picking} suggests this is possible for large networks, but the phenomenon's behavior in the minimal-parameter regime ($<$50K parameters) is less explored.

We study this question on two synthetic tasks chosen for their distinct learning dynamics: (1)~modular addition mod~97, a discrete classification problem requiring compositional generalization, and (2)~random-features linear regression, a continuous task where the optimal solution is linear but the network can overfit. By sweeping sparsity from 0\% to 95\%, we identify the \emph{critical sparsity}---the threshold beyond which performance degrades sharply.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Model Architecture}

We use a 2-layer ReLU MLP: $f(x) = W_2 \, \text{ReLU}(W_1 x + b_1) + b_2$, with hidden dimension $h = 128$. For modular arithmetic, input dimension is $2 \times 97 = 194$ (one-hot encoded), output dimension is 97. For regression, input is $\mathbb{R}^{20}$, output is $\mathbb{R}$.

\subsection{Tasks}

\textbf{Modular arithmetic.} Given one-hot encodings of $a$ and $b$, predict $(a + b) \bmod 97$. All $97^2 = 9{,}409$ pairs are generated; 80\% train, 20\% test. This task requires learning a highly structured mapping.

\textbf{Regression.} Generate $y = Xw^* + \epsilon$ where $X \in \mathbb{R}^{200 \times 20}$, $w^* \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$, $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 0.01 I)$. Split 80/20. Metric: $R^2$.

\subsection{Pruning Strategies}

All pruning is applied \emph{at initialization}, before any training. Pruned weights are held at zero throughout training.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Magnitude pruning}: Remove the smallest-magnitude weights globally across all weight matrices. Biases are not pruned.
    \item \textbf{Random pruning}: Remove weights uniformly at random (seed-controlled).
    \item \textbf{Structured pruning}: Remove entire hidden neurons by L2 norm of incoming weights, zeroing corresponding rows in $W_1$ and columns in $W_2$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

Sparsity levels: $\{0\%, 10\%, 30\%, 50\%, 70\%, 80\%, 90\%, 95\%\}$. Each configuration is run with 3 seeds (42, 123, 7) for variance estimation. Training uses Adam with $\text{lr} = 10^{-2}$ (classification) or $10^{-3}$ (regression), max 500 epochs, early stopping with patience 100. Total: 144 runs.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Accuracy vs.\ Sparsity}

Figure~\ref{fig:sparsity} shows test performance as a function of sparsity for both tasks.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../results/accuracy_vs_sparsity.png}
    \caption{Test accuracy (modular) and $R^2$ (regression) vs.\ sparsity for three pruning strategies. Error bars show $\pm 1$ standard deviation over 3 seeds. Dashed line: critical sparsity (95\% of dense performance) for magnitude pruning.}
    \label{fig:sparsity}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Modular arithmetic.} Magnitude-pruned networks maintain $>$95\% of dense accuracy up to $\sim$70--80\% sparsity, with sharp degradation beyond 90\%. Random pruning degrades earlier, and structured pruning (which removes entire neurons) shows the steepest decline.

\textbf{Regression.} The task is more robust to pruning overall. Magnitude pruning maintains $R^2 > 0.95 \times R^2_{\text{dense}}$ up to $\sim$90\% sparsity, likely because the underlying function is linear and the network is overparameterized relative to the task.

\subsection{Strategy Comparison}

At every sparsity level, magnitude pruning outperforms random pruning, which outperforms structured pruning. The ordering is consistent across both tasks, supporting the hypothesis that initial weight magnitude carries information about a weight's eventual importance---even before training begins.

\subsection{Training Dynamics}

Pruned networks that maintain accuracy often converge in similar or fewer epochs than the dense baseline, consistent with the lottery ticket hypothesis's prediction that winning tickets are ``efficient'' learners.

\section{Discussion}

\textbf{Lottery tickets exist at initialization.} Our results confirm that even in the minimal-parameter regime ($\sim$25K parameters), subnetworks pruned at initialization can match dense performance up to substantial sparsity. This extends the LTH to a regime where overparameterization is modest.

\textbf{Task structure matters.} The critical sparsity is task-dependent: structured tasks (modular arithmetic) are more sensitive to pruning than linear regression, suggesting that lottery tickets in classification tasks may require more precise weight combinations.

\textbf{Magnitude pruning as a prior.} The superiority of magnitude pruning over random pruning implies that the initial weight distribution contains useful signal for identifying important connections---a form of ``information at birth.''

\textbf{Limitations.} We study only 2-layer MLPs on synthetic tasks. Extension to deeper networks, real-world data, and iterative magnitude pruning (the original LTH protocol) would strengthen the conclusions. Our structured pruning is coarse (entire neurons); finer-grained structured pruning may perform better.

\section{Conclusion}

We demonstrate that lottery tickets exist at initialization in tiny neural networks. Magnitude pruning at initialization preserves $>$95\% of dense performance up to 70--90\% sparsity depending on the task, outperforming random and structured pruning. These results suggest that even randomly initialized small networks contain efficient subnetworks, and that weight magnitude at initialization is a meaningful pruning criterion.
